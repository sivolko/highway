(Especially If You're Moving Here for Work)

Let me be honest with you.

When most people Google "what is Bengaluru famous for," they get a listicle with Lalbagh botanical garden, filter coffee, and the phrase "Silicon Valley of India" recycled seventeen different ways. You already know that. You've already read that. That's not why you're here.

You're here because you just got an offer letter. Or maybe you're three weeks from a relocation. Or maybe you simply landed at Kempegowda International Airport at 2 AM, bleary-eyed, staring at a city that feels enormous and unfamiliar and quietly exciting. You need the real version — the one that tells you *what it actually feels like* to live in Bengaluru, not just visit it for a weekend.

So that's what this is. A guide from someone who rides through this city at every hour, eats on every street, and has learned — the hard way — that Bengaluru has layers. Many, many layers.

## First: The One Thing Bengaluru Gets Right That No Other Indian Metro Does

Before anything else, the weather.

Bengaluru sits at roughly 920 metres above sea level. That single geographical fact changes everything. When Delhi is a furnace in May and Mumbai is an airless, humid sauna, Bengaluru is somewhere between 24°C and 32°C with a breeze. Evenings are often cool enough for a light jacket. Mornings are sometimes misty. It rains in proper monsoon fashion — hard and fast and theatrical — and then clears up.

This is not a small thing. This is why people who've lived in Bengaluru and moved away spend years trying to come back. The weather is the city's original promise, and so far, despite everything the urban sprawl has thrown at it, it still mostly delivers.

If you're coming from North India, expect to permanently retire your air conditioner from May through October. If you're from coastal cities, prepare to sleep under a blanket in December. It sounds small. It genuinely changes how much you enjoy being alive here.


## The IT City Thing — And What It Actually Means for You

Yes. Bengaluru is India's technology capital. More than 8 million IT professionals live and work here. Companies from every corner of the planet have offices or R&D centers scattered across the city — Infosys, Wipro, Google, Microsoft, Amazon, Flipkart, and more startups than you could name in a single breath.

But here's what the brochures won't tell you: Bengaluru's tech identity isn't just in the campuses. It has seeped into the *culture* of the city in ways that make it genuinely different from anywhere else in India.

The city has a very high tolerance for ambition. Nobody raises an eyebrow if you're a 24-year-old building a startup in a co-working space. Nobody asks you which caste you're from before they'll talk to you in a café. Nobody cares if you're from Kerala, Bihar, Assam, or Andhra Pradesh — Bengaluru is India's most genuinely cosmopolitan city in that regard. Walk into any neighbourhood restaurant at lunch and you'll hear six different mother tongues at different tables, all somehow comfortable with each other.

That openness is the city's second greatest asset, right after the weather.


## The Neighbourhoods: Where Should You Actually Live?

This is the most important practical question, and it deserves a real answer. Bengaluru is not a city you navigate in the abstract. Where you live *relative to where you work* will determine whether your daily life is manageable or quietly maddening.

Here's the rough map:

**Koramangala** — This is the neighbourhood where the startup ecosystem lives, breathes, and orders food at 11 PM. If you work at a company between Silk Board and the city centre, this is your natural landing zone. It has everything: restaurants for every budget, social infrastructure, good hospitals, and a constant hum of ambition. Rent is on the higher side (₹40,000–70,000 for a decent 2BHK), and parking is a genuine nightmare. But if you want to be *in the thick of it* from day one, Koramangala puts you there. Forum Mall is here. So are a hundred places you'll end up spending Friday nights.

**Indiranagar** — Where people go when they can afford to care about aesthetics. This is the neighbourhood of wide streets (by Bengaluru standards), good restaurants, excellent cafes, and a metro station 350 metres from most residential addresses. It has a slightly older, more settled energy than Koramangala — the kind of place where you meet people who've been in Bengaluru for ten years and decided to stop looking at property listings. Rent is similar or slightly higher than Koramangala. The metro connectivity is its secret advantage.

**Whitefield** — The east side of the city, and Bengaluru's original IT corridor. ITPL is here. A lot of the big tech campuses — TCS, Mindtree, SAP, IBM — are here. Whitefield has grown enormously and now has its own restaurants, malls (VR Bengaluru, Phoenix), and social life. The downside is that it's suburban. Getting to Koramangala or Indiranagar from Whitefield on a Friday night isn't a quick trip. If your office is in Whitefield, live in Whitefield and make peace with it. The metro has a station at Kadugodi now, which helps.

**HSR Layout** — This is the secret best neighbourhood in Bengaluru that not enough people who move here discover early enough. Wide, tree-lined roads. Well-organised sectors. Agara Lake for morning walks. Proximity to the ORR (Outer Ring Road) which connects you to virtually every IT corridor. And a genuine neighbourhood feel — the kind where you develop a relationship with your dosa stall guy within a week. HSR has been called Bengaluru's "startup capital" because of the density of early-stage companies here (Udaan, Cure.Fit, and others started here). Rents are 20–30% lower than Koramangala for similar quality. Highly recommended if you work anywhere on or near the ORR.

**Electronic City** — If your office is in Electronics City Phase 1 or Phase 2 (Infosys, Wipro, HCL campus, and others), the most rational thing you can do is live here. The commute to central Bengaluru used to be brutal, but the Yellow Line of Namma Metro — inaugurated in August 2025 — now connects Electronic City to the rest of the city. RV Road to Bommasandra in 38 minutes. It's changed everything for residents here, and property values along the corridor reflect it.

**A note on commute math:** Google Maps will show you a "20 minute" commute and it will be deeply, tragically optimistic during peak hours (8–10 AM, 5–8 PM). ORR during rush hour is purgatory. Silk Board junction is a famous local trauma. Before you sign any lease, physically test your commute on a weekday morning. This is not optional advice. This is survival advice.

## The Food: The Real Reason People Fall in Love With This City

Bengaluru doesn't have a cuisine. It has *all* the cuisines, done well, in the same square kilometre.

But let's start with what is native — because South Indian breakfast in Bengaluru is one of the finest things to eat in any city on earth, and people who've lived here their whole lives still mean it when they say so.

**The Benne Dosa.** This is the specifically Bengaluru version of a dosa, and it is made with a reckless amount of butter (*benne* in Kannada). Crispy on the outside, soft on the inside, slathered before it reaches you. Central Tiffin Room (CTR) in Malleshwaram — now officially known as Shri Sagar — has been making this since the 1920s. It is still exactly as good as its reputation. Get there before 8:30 AM or queue.

**Vidyarthi Bhavan in Basavanagudi.** Another institution, another masala dosa that has been described in superlatives for decades. The kitchen has not changed. The recipe has not changed. Entry for dine-in closes around 11:30 AM. There is a lesson here about Bengaluru's older self: it rewards the early riser and punishes the person who sleeps in.

**Filter Coffee.** Not the coffee you get at Starbucks. Not the cold brew that's everywhere in Indiranagar. South Indian filter coffee: dark, strong decoction mixed with hot milk, poured back and forth between a steel tumbler and a *dabarah* (a wide-rimmed saucer), frothing and cooling as it goes. Brahmins' Coffee Bar in Basavanagudi is the reference point. It pairs with an idli vada and makes even a difficult morning feel manageable.

**Bisi Bele Bath.** The name translates to "hot lentil rice." It is a Karnataka soul food — rice, lentils, vegetables, ghee, and a particular blend of spices cooked together until they're almost indistinguishable from each other. It is humble and deeply comforting and you will crave it on rainy evenings within the first month of living here.

**Mysore Pak.** Karnataka's primary contribution to Indian dessert culture. A dense, ghee-heavy sweet made of gram flour, with origins in the royal kitchen of Mysore's Maharaja. It should be slightly crispy on the outside and melt inside. Sri Krishna Sweets and Asha Sweet Centre are the names to know.

**The broader picture:** Because Bengaluru has absorbed migrants from across India and across the world, you can find genuinely good Mangalorean seafood (Shetty Lunch Home for ghee roast), Chettinad food, Andhra-style meals, excellent North Indian (the parathas in some of the street corners around Jayanagar at midnight are unexpectedly good), and a Thai/Japanese/Korean restaurant scene that would hold its own in any Southeast Asian city. VV Puram Food Street is where you go on a weekend evening when you can't decide what you want — the street decides for you.

## The Parks: Bengaluru's Unexpected Gift

People don't talk enough about how *green* this city is. Bengaluru reportedly has over 300 parks. Two of them are worth knowing well.

**Lalbagh Botanical Garden** — 240 acres in South Bengaluru, originally commissioned by Hyder Ali in 1760 and completed by Tipu Sultan. It has more than 1,800 species of plants and trees. It has a Victorian-era glasshouse modelled after the Crystal Palace in London. It has a lake where migratory birds show up in season. On a misty January morning, Lalbagh is as close as Bengaluru gets to therapy.

**Cubbon Park** — 300 acres near MG Road, less manicured than Lalbagh but in some ways more interesting for it. Rock outcrops, bamboo clusters, the Gothic-style High Court building visible through the trees. Runners, cyclists, families, and people who come just to sit and exist quietly.

If you work in the city centre, having both of these within a 20-minute auto ride is a significant privilege that you should use more often than you think you will.

## The Culture: Old Bengaluru vs New Bengaluru

There are two Bengalurus that co-exist without fully understanding each other.

**Old Bengaluru** exists in Basavanagudi, Malleswaram, Jayanagar, and Gandhi Bazaar. This is the city of Kannada literature and the *Ganjifa* card painters and the Dodda Ganapathi Temple with its elephant-sized idol. It's the city of the Vidhana Soudha — the impossibly grand state legislature building — and the Bangalore Palace, which looks uncannily like a smaller version of Windsor Castle and was built in 1887 as if the British had decided to prove a point. It's the city of Koshy's, a restaurant on St. Marks Road that has been serving continental and Indian food since the 1940s and still has the atmosphere of a place that has watched the city change from its window and decided not to.

**New Bengaluru** is the city of Indiranagar's 100 Feet Road, where a microbrewery sits next to a sushi restaurant next to a vinyl record store. It is the city of the startup pitch and the hackathon and the Zomato order arriving in 12 minutes. It is the city where a 26-year-old from Manipur and a 28-year-old from Tamil Nadu share a flat in Koramangala and both consider Bengaluru home without having been born here.

The best thing you can do when you move here is spend time in both. The mistake most IT professionals make is staying entirely in New Bengaluru and missing the older city entirely. Basavanagudi on a Sunday morning will show you a Bengaluru that has been there since before the first tech park was built. It's worth the auto ride.

## Kannada: The One Thing You Should Learn Before You Arrive

Bengaluru's official language is Kannada (*ಕನ್ನಡ*), and the people who speak it as their mother tongue are called Kannadigas. The city is cosmopolitan enough that you can survive entirely in Hindi and English, but learning even ten words of Kannada — *"Nanna hesaru [your name]"* (my name is...), *"Swalpa adjust maadi"* (please adjust, a phrase Bengalureans use for everything from squeezing into an auto to asking for patience), *"Bega banni"* (come fast) — will change how local people respond to you.

There is a quiet warmth in Kannada-speaking Bengaluru that opens up when you make any attempt, however imperfect, to meet it halfway.

## Getting Around: The Honest Version

**Namma Metro** is the backbone now. The Purple Line (East-West, from Baiyappanahalli to Kengeri) and the Green Line (North-South, from Nagasandra to Silk Board) have been running for years. The **Yellow Line** — opened August 2025, running 16 elevated stations from RV Road to Bommasandra — has been the most significant addition in years, finally connecting South Bengaluru and Electronic City to the main network properly. Ride it if you can.

**Autos (auto-rickshaws)** — The three-wheeled lifeline of the city. They officially run on meters, though negotiating a fixed price is common. The Namma Yatri and Rapido apps have made this significantly less stressful — you book, the price is set, the driver comes. Recommended for short hops where the metro doesn't reach.

**Ola and Uber** work reliably. Surge pricing during rain is a specific kind of Bengaluru suffering you will come to know.

**Your own vehicle** — If you're working in a location that's metro-distant, a two-wheeler changes your life in this city. A scooter or a commuter motorcycle gets you through gaps in traffic that no car can reach. Traffic on ORR and Silk Board during peak hours is legitimately bad — the kind of bad where you stop, and then stop again, and then continue stopping for 45 minutes. A good two-wheeler (or the metro) is the answer to this.

## Day Trips: Because Bengaluru Is a Gateway, Not Just a Destination

Once you settle in, the city's second secret emerges: its geography puts you within reach of some extraordinary places.

**Nandi Hills** — 60 kilometres from the city. A sunrise here, on a clear morning, with the plains spread below you and mist in the valleys, is worth waking up at 4:30 AM for. The road up is excellent for a motorcycle ride.

**Coorg (Kodagu)** — 250 kilometres. Coffee plantations, waterfalls, the Tadiandamol trek, rain and mist in the right months. A weekend here feels like pressing reset on whatever the city has been doing to your stress levels.

**Sakleshpur** — 220 kilometres. Known for the heritage railway trek through Western Ghats forest. Quiet, green, and absolutely worth two days.

**Chikmagalur** — 240 kilometres. The coffee capital of Karnataka. Colonial bungalows, Mullayanagiri (the highest peak in the state), and the kind of morning mist that makes you reach for your camera before you've had tea.

These are not just weekend options. If you move to Bengaluru and never explore its surroundings, you've only lived in half the city.

## The Practical Stuff Nobody Puts in Travel Guides

A few things that matter more than they seem:

**Rain and flooding.** Bengaluru can flood, especially in low-lying areas like parts of Marathahalli, Bellandur, and Whitefield during heavy rains. This has improved in some areas and hasn't in others. Ask about this before signing a lease.

**Water supply.** Not every apartment in Bengaluru has reliable BWSSB (municipal) water. Many buildings depend on water tankers. Ask your potential landlord specifically about this. It matters daily.

**The auto/cab drivers** — Most are fair and will help you if you look genuinely lost. Learn to read the auto meter if you're not using an app. The standard rate is ₹30 for the first kilometre and approximately ₹15 per km after that.

**Paying rent** — Most landlords in Bengaluru want 10 months of security deposit along with 11-month lease agreements. Yes, ten months. It's a uniquely Bengaluru number and it can come as a shock if you're coming from another city.

**SIM card** — Get an Airtel or Jio connection. Jio has the widest 4G/5G coverage across the tech corridors. Some buildings in Whitefield had notoriously bad signal from certain carriers — ask residents before you pick.

**Learning to say "traffic" as an explanation for everything.** This is cultural integration. When someone is 40 minutes late in Bengaluru, the word "traffic" is both an explanation and a full emotional narrative. You will soon use it exactly the same way.

## What Nobody Told Me Before I Came Here

Bengaluru is a city that grows on you, not a city that hits you immediately. Mumbai hits you in the face from the moment the taxi leaves the airport. Delhi announces itself. Bengaluru does neither of those things. It is quieter on the surface. More understated.

But then one morning you're at a dosa place in Basavanagudi at 7 AM and the filter coffee arrives in a steel tumbler and the ceiling fans are turning slowly and the auto horns are audible from outside and you realise you know exactly where you are, and that you like being here, and that this city has done something to you that isn't quite love yet but is moving toward it.

That's the Bengaluru thing. It doesn't demand you love it. It just keeps being itself until you do.
